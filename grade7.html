Unit Rates with Fractions

NY State Standard: 7.RP.1

📌 Learning Objective

Students will compute unit rates associated with ratios of fractions, including ratios of lengths, areas, and other quantities measured in like or different units.

Unit Rates with Complex Fractions

In Grade 6, you learned unit rates like "60 miles per hour." In Grade 7, unit rates can involve fractions divided by fractions — these are called complex fractions.

What is a unit rate? A unit rate compares a quantity to exactly 1 unit of something else. The word "per" signals a unit rate: miles per hour, cups per batch, dollars per pound.

How to Compute Unit Rates with Fractions

If someone walks ½ mile in ¼ hour, the unit rate is:

½ ÷ ¼ = ½ × 4/1 = 2 miles per hour

The method is always the same: divide the first quantity by the second using Keep-Change-Flip (KCF).

Keep-Change-Flip Review

Keep the first fraction. Change division to multiplication. Flip the second fraction (take its reciprocal).

¾ ÷ ⅔ = ¾ × 3/2 = 9/8 = 1 ⅛

Common mistake: Students sometimes flip the FIRST fraction instead of the second. Always flip the one AFTER the division sign!

📝 Worked Example: Unit Rate with Fractions

A recipe uses ¾ cup of sugar for every ½ batch. How much sugar per 1 full batch?

  1. Set up the division: ¾ ÷ ½.
  2. Keep-Change-Flip: ¾ × 2/1 = 6/4.
  3. Simplify: 6/4 = 1 ½ cups per batch.
  4. Check: Does this make sense? Half a batch uses ¾ cup, so a full batch should use more. 1½ > ¾. ✅
💡 Remember: To find a unit rate with fractions, divide the numerator quantity by the denominator quantity using Keep-Change-Flip. Always simplify your answer!

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: A runner goes ⅔ mile in ⅙ hour. Speed in mph?

⅔ ÷ ⅙ = ⅔ × 6 = 4 mph

Example 2: ¾ pound of cheese costs $½. Price per pound?

½ ÷ ¾ = ½ × 4/3 = $⅔ per pound

Example 3: A painter covers ⅖ of a wall in ⅓ hour. Rate?

⅖ ÷ ⅓ = ⅖ × 3 = 6/5 walls per hour

Example 4: ⅜ of a tank fills in ¼ hour. Rate per hour?

⅜ ÷ ¼ = ⅜ × 4 = 1½ tanks per hour

Example 5: A snail goes ⅙ mile in ⅔ hour. Speed?

⅙ ÷ ⅔ = ⅙ × 3/2 = 3/12 = ¼ mph

Proportional Relationships in Tables

NY State Standard: 7.RP.2a

📌 Learning Objective

Students will determine whether two quantities are in a proportional relationship by testing for equivalent ratios in a table.

Identifying Proportional Relationships

Two quantities are proportional if every pair in the table forms an equivalent ratio. This means the relationship is always "multiply by the same number."

The y/x Test

For each row in a table, compute y ÷ x. If you get the same value every time, the relationship is proportional. That constant value is called k, the constant of proportionality.

Proportional example: x: 2, 4, 6 → y: 6, 12, 18. Check: 6/2 = 3, 12/4 = 3, 18/6 = 3. All equal to 3 → Proportional with k = 3.

Not proportional: x: 1, 2, 3 → y: 3, 5, 7. Check: 3/1 = 3, 5/2 = 2.5, 7/3 ≈ 2.33. Not all equal → Not proportional.

Key Characteristics

A proportional relationship always passes through (0, 0). If when x = 0, y ≠ 0, it is NOT proportional.

Common mistake: A linear relationship (like y = 2x + 1) is NOT proportional even though it forms a straight line, because it does not pass through the origin.

Real-world clue: If there is a starting fee, initial amount, or base cost, it is usually NOT proportional.

📝 Worked Example: Testing a Table

Is this proportional? Hours worked: 1, 2, 3, 4. Pay: $8, $16, $24, $32.

  1. Divide y/x for each row: 8/1 = 8, 16/2 = 8, 24/3 = 8, 32/4 = 8.
  2. All ratios equal 8.
  3. Answer: Yes, proportional with k = $8/hour.
  4. Note: When hours = 0, pay = $0 (no work, no pay). Passes through (0,0). ✅
💡 Remember: If y/x is the same for every row AND the relationship passes through (0,0), it is proportional!

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: x: 3, 6, 9. y: 12, 24, 36. Proportional?

12/3=4, 24/6=4, 36/9=4. Yes, k=4

Example 2: x: 2, 4, 5. y: 6, 12, 16. Proportional?

6/2=3, 12/4=3, 16/5=3.2. No

Example 3: x: 5, 10, 15. y: 1, 2, 3. Proportional?

1/5=0.2, 2/10=0.2, 3/15=0.2. Yes, k=0.2

Example 4: x: 0, 1, 2. y: 5, 8, 11. Proportional?

When x=0, y=5 (not 0). No — does not pass through origin

Example 5: Taxi: $3 base + $2/mile. Miles: 1,2,3. Cost: 5,7,9. Proportional?

5/1=5, 7/2=3.5. No — the base fee breaks proportionality

Lesson 3 • Ratios & Proportional Relationships

Graphing Proportional Relationships

NY State Standard: 7.RP.2d

📌 Learning Objective

Students will graph proportional relationships and explain what points (x,y) mean, especially (0,0) and (1,r).

Graphs of Proportional Relationships

Every proportional relationship graphs as a straight line through the origin (0,0).

Key Points to Know

(0, 0): When there is no input, there is no output. Zero hours of work = $0 pay. Zero gallons = zero miles. This MUST be true for a proportional relationship.

(1, r): The point where x = 1 gives you the unit rate directly. If the line passes through (1, 3), that means 3 units of y for every 1 unit of x — the unit rate is 3.

Proportional vs. Linear (Not Proportional)

Both make straight lines, but only proportional lines pass through (0,0).

y = 3x is proportional (passes through origin). y = 3x + 2 is linear but NOT proportional (y-intercept is 2, not 0).

Finding the Unit Rate from a Graph

Pick any point (x, y) on the line and divide: k = y/x. Or simply read the y-value when x = 1.

If the line goes through (4, 12), then k = 12/4 = 3. The equation is y = 3x.

📝 Worked Example: Interpreting a Graph

A graph shows pounds of apples (x) and cost (y). The line passes through (0,0) and (1, 2.50).

  1. (0, 0): Zero pounds costs $0. This confirms proportionality.
  2. (1, 2.50): The unit rate is $2.50 per pound.
  3. Equation: y = 2.50x.
  4. Prediction: 6 pounds would cost 2.50(6) = $15.00.
💡 Remember: A proportional graph is always a straight line through (0,0). The y-value at x=1 IS the unit rate!

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: Graph passes through (0,0) and (4,12). Unit rate?

12/4 = 3

Example 2: A line goes through (0,2) and (1,5). Proportional?

No (y-intercept is 2, not 0)

Example 3: Point (1, 7) is on a proportional line. Equation?

y = 7x

Example 4: Line through (0,0) and (5,20). What is y when x=8?

k=4, so y = 4(8) = 32

Example 5: Two lines: y=4x and y=4x+3. Which is proportional?

y = 4x (passes through origin)

Lesson 4 • Ratios & Proportional Relationships

Constant of Proportionality

NY State Standard: 7.RP.2b

📌 Learning Objective

Students will identify the constant of proportionality (k) in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.

The Constant of Proportionality (k)

The constant of proportionality k is the value that connects x and y in a proportional relationship: y = kx. It is the same as the unit rate.

Finding k in Different Representations

In a table: Divide any y by its x. k = y/x. Check multiple rows to confirm.

In a graph: k is the slope (rise/run). Or read the y-value at x = 1.

In an equation y = kx: k is the number in front of x (the coefficient).

In words: Look for "per" or "each" or "every." "$3 per pound" means k = 3. "15 miles every hour" means k = 15.

Important Notes

k is always the same value no matter which point you use. If different points give different k-values, the relationship is NOT proportional.

k can be a fraction or decimal: if 3 items cost $2, then k = 2/3 ≈ $0.67 per item.

The units of k tell you what it means: dollars per pound, miles per hour, etc.

📝 Worked Example: Finding k

A table shows: x: 2, 5, 8 and y: 7, 17.5, 28. Find k and write the equation.

  1. Divide: y/x = 7/2 = 3.5.
  2. Check: 17.5/5 = 3.5 ✅. 28/8 = 3.5 ✅.
  3. k = 3.5. Equation: y = 3.5x.
  4. Meaning: y increases by 3.5 for every 1 unit increase in x.
💡 Remember: k = y/x for any pair. Once you know k, the equation is y = kx. Always check with multiple points!

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: Equation y = 4x. What is k?

k = 4

Example 2: A car goes 180 miles in 3 hours. k?

180/3 = 60 mph

Example 3: Graph passes through (5, 20). k?

20/5 = 4

Example 4: A recipe says 2 cups water per 3 cups rice. k for water per rice?

2/3 ≈ 0.67

Example 5: If k = 0.5, what does that mean?

y is always half of x

Lesson 5 • Ratios & Proportional Relationships

Equations for Proportions

NY State Standard: 7.RP.2c

📌 Learning Objective

Students will represent proportional relationships using equations of the form y = kx and use them to solve problems.

Writing Proportional Equations

Every proportional relationship can be written as y = kx, where k is the constant of proportionality (unit rate).

Steps to Write the Equation

1. Find k: Divide any y-value by its corresponding x-value.

2. Write: y = kx (or use meaningful variable names like C = 0.60p for cost and pounds).

3. Use it: Plug in values to find unknowns.

Using the Equation to Solve Problems

Find y given x: Just multiply. If y = 5x and x = 12, then y = 5(12) = 60.

Find x given y: Divide. If y = 5x and y = 75, then 75 = 5x, so x = 75/5 = 15.

Real-World Example

Bananas cost $0.60 per pound. Equation: C = 0.60p.

Cost of 5 pounds: C = 0.60(5) = $3.00. How many pounds for $9? 9 = 0.60p, p = 15 pounds.

📝 Worked Example: Writing an Equation

A machine fills 12 bottles every 4 minutes. Write an equation and find bottles in 15 minutes.

  1. Find k: 12 bottles / 4 minutes = 3 bottles per minute.
  2. Equation: b = 3m (bottles = 3 × minutes).
  3. At 15 min: b = 3(15) = 45 bottles.
  4. Reverse: How long for 120 bottles? 120 = 3m, m = 40 minutes.
💡 Remember: Find k first, write y = kx, then plug in to solve for unknowns. You can find EITHER variable!

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: k = 5. Write equation.

y = 5x

Example 2: y = 8x. Find y when x = 7.

8(7) = 56

Example 3: $24 for 6 items. Equation?

C = 4n ($4 per item)

Example 4: y = 3x. Find x when y = 45.

45 = 3x, x = 15

Example 5: A car gets 30 mpg. Equation and gallons for 450 miles?

m = 30g. 450 = 30g, g = 15 gallons

Percent Problems

NY State Standard: 7.RP.3

📌 Learning Objective

Students will solve multi-step percent problems including tax, tip, markup, discount, percent increase, percent decrease, and percent error.

Multi-Step Percent Problems

Percent problems show up everywhere: shopping, taxes, tips, interest, and data analysis.

Tax, Tip, and Markup (Adding a Percent)

Method 1: Calculate the percent amount, then add. 15% tip on $40: $40 × 0.15 = $6. Total: $40 + $6 = $46.

Method 2 (Shortcut): Multiply by (1 + rate). $40 × 1.15 = $46. This is faster!

Discount and Markdown (Subtracting a Percent)

Method 1: Calculate then subtract. 20% off $80: $80 × 0.20 = $16. Sale: $80 - $16 = $64.

Shortcut: Multiply by (1 - rate). $80 × 0.80 = $64.

Percent Change (Increase or Decrease)

Percent Change = (New - Original) / Original × 100%

Increase from 50 to 65: (65-50)/50 = 15/50 = 0.30 = 30% increase.

Decrease from 80 to 60: (60-80)/80 = -20/80 = -0.25 = 25% decrease.

Common Mistakes

Always calculate percent on the ORIGINAL amount, not the new amount (unless told otherwise).

Discount THEN tax is different from tax THEN discount. Read carefully!

📝 Worked Example: Tax + Tip Problem

A meal costs $35. Tax is 8% and you leave a 20% tip on the pre-tax amount. What is the total?

  1. Tax: $35 × 0.08 = $2.80.
  2. Tip: $35 × 0.20 = $7.00 (tip on original, not taxed amount).
  3. Total: $35 + $2.80 + $7.00 = $44.80.
  4. Why tip on pre-tax? That is the standard practice — you tip on the food cost, not the tax.
💡 Remember: For tax/tip: calculate each percent on the ORIGINAL amount, then add everything together. Use the shortcut multiplier when possible!

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: 25% off a $60 jacket?

Shortcut: $60 × 0.75 = $45

Example 2: Price went from $20 to $25. Percent increase?

(25-20)/20 = 5/20 = 25% increase

Example 3: 6% tax on $150?

$150 × 1.06 = $159

Example 4: Price dropped from $100 to $72. Percent decrease?

(72-100)/100 = -28/100 = 28% decrease

Example 5: 20% off then 10% off $100. Final price?

$100 × 0.80 = $80. $80 × 0.90 = $72 (NOT 30% off!)

Lesson 7 • The Number System

Adding Integers

NY State Standard: 7.NS.1

📌 Learning Objective

Students will add positive and negative integers using number lines and rules, and interpret sums in real-world contexts.

Adding Integers

Integers are whole numbers and their negatives: ..., -3, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, 3, ...

Rules for Adding Integers

Same signs: Add the absolute values, keep the shared sign.

(-5) + (-3) = -8 (both negative, add 5+3, keep negative)

4 + 7 = 11 (both positive, add, keep positive)

Different signs: Subtract the smaller absolute value from the larger. The result takes the sign of the number with the larger absolute value.

(-8) + 5 = -3 (|8| > |5|, so the answer is negative. 8-5=3.)

7 + (-4) = 3 (|7| > |4|, so positive. 7-4=3.)

Additive Inverse

Every number has an opposite. A number plus its opposite always equals zero: 5 + (-5) = 0. This is the additive inverse property.

Real-World Connections

Temperature: "It was -5°F and rose 8 degrees" = -5 + 8 = 3°F.

Money: "Owe $12, earn $7" = -12 + 7 = -$5 (still in debt).

📝 Worked Example: Adding Integers

Solve: (-12) + 7

  1. Different signs: Subtract absolute values: 12 - 7 = 5.
  2. Larger absolute value is 12 (from the -12), so the answer is negative.
  3. Answer: -5.
  4. Real-world check: If you owe $12 and earn $7, you still owe $5.
💡 Remember: Same signs = add and keep sign. Different signs = subtract and take the sign of the bigger absolute value!

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: (-6) + (-9) = ?

Same sign (both negative): 6+9 = 15. -15

Example 2: 15 + (-15) = ?

Additive inverses: 0

Example 3: (-3) + 10 = ?

Different signs: 10-3 = 7, positive wins. 7

Example 4: (-25) + (-5) = ?

Same sign: 25+5 = 30. -30

Example 5: 100 + (-99) = ?

Different: 100-99 = 1. Positive wins. 1

Lesson 8 • The Number System

Subtracting Integers

NY State Standard: 7.NS.1c

📌 Learning Objective

Students will subtract integers by adding the additive inverse: p - q = p + (-q), and find distances on the number line.

Subtracting Integers

The golden rule: Subtracting is the same as adding the opposite.

p - q = p + (-q)

Change every subtraction to adding the opposite, then use your addition rules!

Examples Broken Down

5 - 8: Change to 5 + (-8). Different signs: 8-5=3, negative wins. -3.

(-4) - 3: Change to (-4) + (-3). Same signs: 4+3=7, both negative. -7.

(-6) - (-2): Change to (-6) + (+2). Different signs: 6-2=4, negative wins. -4.

Key insight: Subtracting a negative = adding a positive! (-6) - (-2) = (-6) + 2.

Distance Between Two Numbers

Distance = |a - b| (absolute value of the difference). Distance is always positive.

Distance between -3 and 4: |(-3) - 4| = |-7| = 7 units.

Distance between 2 and 9: |2 - 9| = |-7| = 7 units.

📝 Worked Example: Subtracting Integers

Solve: (-9) - (-4)

  1. Change subtraction to addition: (-9) + (+4).
  2. Now use addition rules: Different signs. |9| > |4|.
  3. Subtract: 9 - 4 = 5. Negative wins.
  4. Answer: -5.
💡 Remember: Always change subtraction to adding the opposite FIRST. Then apply your addition rules. Subtracting a negative = adding a positive!

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: 7 - 12 = ?

7 + (-12) = -5

Example 2: (-5) - (-8) = ?

(-5) + (+8) = 3

Example 3: Distance between -3 and 4?

|(-3) - 4| = |-7| = 7

Example 4: 0 - (-10) = ?

0 + 10 = 10

Example 5: (-20) - (-20) = ?

(-20) + 20 = 0

Multiplying & Dividing Integers

NY State Standard: 7.NS.2a

📌 Learning Objective

Students will apply sign rules to multiply and divide positive and negative integers, and interpret products and quotients in real-world contexts.

Multiplying & Dividing Integers

The sign rules for multiplication and division are the same:

Same Signs → POSITIVE Result

(+) × (+) = +   |   (-) × (-) = +

3 × 5 = 15   |   (-4) × (-6) = 24

Why is negative × negative = positive? Think of it as "the opposite of a loss is a gain."

Different Signs → NEGATIVE Result

(+) × (-) = -   |   (-) × (+) = -

5 × (-3) = -15   |   (-8) ÷ 2 = -4

Multiple Factors

Count the negative signs: even count = positive, odd count = negative.

(-2)(-3)(-4) = three negatives (odd) = -24.

(-1)(-2)(-3)(-4) = four negatives (even) = +24.

Special Cases

Anything × 0 = 0.    0 ÷ (anything except 0) = 0.    Dividing by 0 is undefined.

📝 Worked Example: Sign Rules

Solve: (-8) × (-5)

  1. Two negatives: Same signs = positive result.
  2. Multiply the values: 8 × 5 = 40.
  3. Answer: +40.
  4. Count check: 2 negatives (even count) = positive. ✅
💡 Remember: Same signs = positive. Different signs = negative. Count negatives for multiple factors: even = positive, odd = negative!

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: (-7) × 3 = ?

Different signs: -21

Example 2: (-20) ÷ (-4) = ?

Same signs: +5

Example 3: (-2)(-3)(-1) = ?

3 negatives (odd): -6

Example 4: 0 × (-500) = ?

0 (anything times 0)

Example 5: (-1)^4 = ?

Even power: +1

Adding & Subtracting Rational Numbers

NY State Standard: 7.NS.1d

📌 Learning Objective

Students will add and subtract fractions, decimals, and mixed numbers with different signs.

Rational Number Addition & Subtraction

All the integer rules you learned apply to fractions and decimals too!

With Decimals

Use the same sign rules. Line up decimal points and compute.

-3.5 + 1.2: Different signs. |3.5| > |1.2|. 3.5 - 1.2 = 2.3. Negative wins: -2.3.

-4.6 - 2.1 = -4.6 + (-2.1): Same signs. 4.6 + 2.1 = 6.7. Both negative: -6.7.

With Fractions

Step 1: Find a common denominator.

Step 2: Apply integer sign rules to the numerators.

-⅓ + ¾: LCD = 12. -4/12 + 9/12 = 5/12 (|9| > |4|, positive wins).

-⅖ - ⅕ = -⅖ + (-⅕) = -⅗ (same signs, add, keep negative).

Common Mistakes

Forgetting to find a common denominator before adding fractions with different signs.

Getting confused by double negatives: -(-½) = +½.

📝 Worked Example: Fraction Operations

Solve: -¾ + ½

  1. Common denominator: LCD = 4.
  2. Convert: -3/4 + 2/4.
  3. Different signs: |3| > |2|, negative wins.
  4. Subtract numerators: 3 - 2 = 1.
  5. Answer: -¼.
💡 Remember: Same rules as integers! Just find a common denominator first for fractions. For decimals, line up the decimal points.

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: -2.7 + 4.1 = ?

Different signs: 4.1-2.7 = 1.4

Example 2: -½ - ⅓ = ?

-3/6 + (-2/6) = -5/6

Example 3: ⅝ + (-⅜) = ?

Different signs: 5-3 = 2/8 = ¼

Example 4: -1.5 + (-3.5) = ?

Same signs: 1.5+3.5 = 5. -5

Example 5: -(⅔) - (-⅓) = ?

-⅔ + ⅓ = -⅓

Multiplying & Dividing Rational Numbers

NY State Standard: 7.NS.2c

📌 Learning Objective

Students will multiply and divide fractions, decimals, and mixed numbers with different signs.

Multiplying & Dividing Rational Numbers

The same sign rules from integers apply to ALL rational numbers (fractions and decimals).

Multiplying Fractions with Signs

1. Determine the sign first (same = positive, different = negative).

NY State Standard: 7.NS.2d

📌 Learning Objective

Fractions as Decimals

To convert any fraction to a decimal, divide the numerator by the denominator.

Terminating Decimals

These END. The division comes out evenly.

⅜ = 3 ÷ 8 = 0.375   |   ¼ = 1 ÷ 4 = 0.25

How to tell: If the denominator (in lowest terms) has ONLY factors of 2 and/or 5, the decimal terminates.

8 = 2×2×2 (only 2s) → terminates. 20 = 4×5 (only 2s and 5s) → terminates.

Repeating Decimals

These go on forever with a repeating pattern. Write a bar over the repeating digits.

⅓ = 0.333... = 0.3̅   |   2/11 = 0.181818... = 0.1̅8̅

How to tell: If the denominator has ANY prime factor besides 2 or 5, it repeats.

3 has factor 3 (not 2 or 5) → repeats. 6 = 2×3 (has a 3) → repeats.

Common Conversions to Memorize

½=0.5, ⅓≈0.33, ¼=0.25, ⅕=0.2, ⅙≈0.167, ⅛=0.125

📝 Worked Example: Converting

  2. Long division: 6 goes into 50 eight times (48), remainder 2. 6 into 20 three times (18), remainder 2. Pattern repeats!
  3. 5 ÷ 6 = 0.8333...
  4. Answer: 0.83̅ (the 3 repeats).
💡 Remember: Divide numerator by denominator. If it ends, it terminates. If a pattern repeats, put a bar over the repeating digits!

NY State Standard: 7.EE.1

📌 Learning Objective

Simplifying Expressions

An expression combines numbers, variables, and operations. Simplifying means making it shorter while keeping the same value.

Like Terms

Like terms have the SAME variable raised to the SAME power.

3x and 5x are like terms (both have x).   3x and 3y are NOT (different variables).   2x and 2x² are NOT (different powers).

Combine by adding/subtracting coefficients: 4x + 3x = 7x.   2y - 5y = -3y.   8 + (-3) = 5 (constants are like terms too).

Expanding (Distributive Property)

a(b + c) = ab + ac. Multiply the outside term by EVERY term inside.

3(x + 4) = 3x + 12.    -2(5x - 3) = -10x + 6.    ½(6x - 8) = 3x - 4.

Watch negatives! -2(5x - 3): negative times negative 3 gives POSITIVE 6.

Factoring (Reverse of Expanding)

Pull out the GCF (greatest common factor).

6x + 9 = 3(2x + 3).    10x - 15 = 5(2x - 3).    -4x + 8 = -4(x - 2).

📝 Worked Example: Expanding and Combining

Simplify: 2(3x + 4) - 5x

  1. Distribute the 2: 2(3x) + 2(4) = 6x + 8.
  2. Write full expression: 6x + 8 - 5x.
  3. Combine like terms: (6x - 5x) + 8 = x + 8.
  4. Answer: x + 8.
💡 Remember: Distribute first, then combine like terms. Watch negative signs carefully when distributing!

Rewriting Expressions

NY State Standard: 7.EE.2

📌 Learning Objective

Rewriting Expressions to Reveal Meaning

The same situation can be expressed in equivalent but different forms. Different forms reveal different things.

Percent Increase (Tax, Tip, Markup)

Price p with 5% increase:

Form 1: p + 0.05p (original plus increase — shows the two parts separately)

Form 2: 1.05p (combined multiplier — shows total is 105% of original)

Both are equivalent: p + 0.05p = (1 + 0.05)p = 1.05p.

Percent Decrease (Discount, Depreciation)

Price p with 20% discount:

Form 1: p - 0.20p (original minus discount)

Form 2: 0.80p (you pay 80% of the original)

Why This Matters

The rewritten form 0.80p immediately tells you the sale price is 80% of the original — you do not need to calculate the discount separately. Similarly, 1.08p tells you the total with 8% tax is 108% of the price.

Being able to see both forms helps you solve problems faster and understand what is really happening.

📝 Worked Example: Rewriting

An item costs d dollars. After a 7% tax, what is the total? Write two equivalent expressions.

  1. Form 1: d + 0.07d (price plus tax amount).
  2. Form 2: 1.07d (combined — total is 107% of price).
  3. Both are equivalent: d + 0.07d = (1 + 0.07)d = 1.07d.
  4. Form 2 is simpler for calculations.
💡 Remember: Rewriting reveals meaning: 1.07d shows the total is 107% of the original price. The multiplier form is usually faster for calculations!

Solving Equations (px + q = r)

NY State Standard: 7.EE.4a

📌 Learning Objective

Solving Two-Step Equations

A two-step equation requires two operations to isolate x. The key is to undo operations in reverse order.

Form: px + q = r

Step 1: Undo addition/subtraction first (isolate the term with x).

Step 2: Undo multiplication/division (isolate x itself).

Example: 3x + 7 = 22.

Step 1: 3x = 22 - 7 = 15.    Step 2: x = 15/3 = 5.

Form: p(x + q) = r

Option A: Distribute first, then solve the two-step equation.

Option B: Divide both sides by p first, then solve one-step.

Example: 5(x + 2) = 35.

Option A: 5x + 10 = 35 → 5x = 25 → x = 5.

Option B: x + 2 = 7 → x = 5. (Faster!)

Always Check Your Answer!

Plug x back into the ORIGINAL equation. Both sides should be equal.

Check x=5 in 3x+7=22: 3(5)+7 = 15+7 = 22. ✅

📝 Worked Example: Two-Step Equation

Solve: 4x - 9 = 15

  1. Step 1 — Add 9: 4x = 15 + 9 = 24.
  2. Step 2 — Divide by 4: x = 24/4 = 6.
  3. Check: 4(6) - 9 = 24 - 9 = 15. ✅
💡 Remember: Undo addition/subtraction first, then multiplication/division. ALWAYS check your answer by plugging back in!

Solving & Graphing Inequalities

NY State Standard: 7.EE.4b

📌 Learning Objective

Solving and Graphing Inequalities

Inequalities are like equations, but instead of = you have <, >, ≤, or ≥.

Solving: Same as Equations with ONE Exception

Solve exactly like equations, step by step. BUT:

When you multiply or divide by a NEGATIVE number, FLIP the inequality sign!

Example: 2x + 3 > 11 → 2x > 8 → x > 4. (No flip needed — divided by positive 2.)

Example: -3x + 6 ≤ 12 → -3x ≤ 6 → x ≥ -2. (Flipped! Divided by -3.)

Graphing on a Number Line

Open circle (ˆ): For < or > (NOT including the endpoint).

Closed circle (•): For ≤ or ≥ (INCLUDING the endpoint).

Arrow direction: Points toward the solutions. x > 4 → arrow goes right. x < 2 → arrow goes left.

Interpreting Solutions

"You need at least $100" means earnings ≥ 100. "No more than 5 items" means items ≤ 5.

📝 Worked Example: Solving Inequalities

A salesperson earns $50/week plus $3 per sale. They want at least $100 this week. How many sales?

  1. Inequality: 50 + 3s ≥ 100.
  2. Subtract 50: 3s ≥ 50.
  3. Divide by 3: s ≥ 50/3 ≈ 16.67.
  4. Since sales must be whole: s ≥ 17 sales.
  5. Graph: Closed circle at 17, arrow going right.
💡 Remember: Flip the sign ONLY when multiplying/dividing by a negative. Open circle for < or >, closed for ≤ or ≥!

Circles: Circumference & Area

NY State Standard: 7.G.4

📌 Learning Objective

Circle Formulas

Key Vocabulary

Radius (r): Distance from the center to the edge. Diameter (d): Distance across through the center. d = 2r.

π (pi): The ratio of circumference to diameter for ANY circle. π ≈ 3.14 or 22/7.

Circumference (Perimeter of a Circle)

C = πd = 2πr

Circumference measures around the circle (like wrapping a string around it).

Example: r = 7 cm. C = 2(3.14)(7) = 43.96 cm.

Area (Space Inside)

A = πr²

Area measures the space inside the circle (like how much paint to fill it).

Example: r = 7 cm. A = 3.14(7²) = 3.14(49) = 153.86 cm².

Common Mistakes

Using diameter instead of radius in the area formula. Always convert d to r first: r = d/2.

Forgetting to square the radius. πr² means π × r × r, NOT π × 2r.

📝 Worked Example: Circle Calculations

  1. Find radius: r = 10/2 = 5 cm.
  2. Circumference: C = πd = 3.14(10) = 31.4 cm.
  3. Area: A = πr² = 3.14(5²) = 3.14(25) = 78.5 cm².
  4. Units: Circumference is in cm (length). Area is in cm² (square units).
💡 Remember: C = πd or 2πr (around the outside). A = πr² (inside space). Always use RADIUS for area — convert diameter first!

Angle Relationships

Types of Angle Pairs

Complementary: Two angles that add to 90°. If one is 35°, the other is 90-35 = 55°.

Supplementary: Two angles that add to 180°. If one is 120°, the other is 180-120 = 60°.

Vertical angles: Formed by two intersecting lines, across from each other. They are always equal.

Adjacent angles: Share a side and vertex. They sit next to each other.

Using Algebra to Find Unknown Angles

When angles have expressions (like 2x+10), set up an equation using the relationship:

Complementary: angle1 + angle2 = 90

Supplementary: angle1 + angle2 = 180

Vertical: angle1 = angle2

Example

x + (3x+20) = 180 → 4x + 20 = 180 → 4x = 160 → x = 40.

Angles: 40° and 140°. Check: 40+140 = 180. ✅

  4. Angles: 2(30)+10 = 70° and 3(30)+20 = 110°.
  5. Check: 70 + 110 = 180. ✅

Lesson 24 • Statistics & Probability

Box Plots & Interquartile Range

NY State Standard: 7.SP.1

📌 Learning Objective

Students will construct and interpret box plots, find the interquartile range, and determine if a data point is an outlier.

Box Plots and IQR

A box plot (box-and-whisker) displays a data set using the five-number summary:

Minimum → Q1 → Median (Q2) → Q3 → Maximum

How to Find the Five-Number Summary

1. Order data from least to greatest.

2. Median (Q2): Middle value. If even count, average the two middle values.

3. Q1: Median of the lower half (values below Q2).

4. Q3: Median of the upper half (values above Q2).

5. Min and Max are the smallest and largest values.

Interquartile Range (IQR)

IQR = Q3 - Q1. This measures the spread of the middle 50% of data.

A smaller IQR means data is more tightly clustered. A larger IQR means more spread out.

Identifying Outliers

A value is an outlier if it falls below Q1 - 1.5(IQR) or above Q3 + 1.5(IQR).

Example: Q1=10, Q3=20, IQR=10. Lower fence: 10-15=-5. Upper fence: 20+15=35. Any value below -5 or above 35 is an outlier.

📝 Worked Example: Finding IQR

Data: 2, 5, 7, 8, 10, 12, 15. Find the five-number summary and IQR.

  1. Already ordered. Min = 2, Max = 15.
  2. Median (Q2): 8 (middle of 7 values).
  3. Lower half: 2, 5, 7. Q1 = 5.
  4. Upper half: 10, 12, 15. Q3 = 12.
  5. IQR = Q3 - Q1 = 12 - 5 = 7.
💡 Remember: Order the data first! Q1 = median of lower half, Q3 = median of upper half. IQR = Q3 - Q1. For outliers: check 1.5 × IQR above Q3 and below Q1.

Comparing Data Distributions

NY State Standard: 7.SP.3

📌 Learning Objective

Students will informally compare two data distributions using measures of center (mean, median) and variability (range, IQR).

Comparing Distributions

To compare two data sets, look at two things: center and spread.

Measures of Center

Mean: Add all values, divide by count. Affected by outliers.

Median: Middle value. More resistant to outliers.

Mode: Most frequent value.

Compare: Which group has a higher or lower average?

Measures of Spread (Variability)

Range: Max - Min. Simple but affected by outliers.

IQR: Q3 - Q1. More reliable measure of typical spread.

Compare: Which group is more consistent (smaller spread) or more variable (larger spread)?

Visual Overlap of Box Plots

If two box plots barely overlap, the groups are quite different.

If they overlap a lot, the groups are similar.

Making Comparisons

Always discuss BOTH center AND spread. Example: "Class A has a higher median score (85 vs 78), but Class B has a smaller IQR (8 vs 14), meaning Class B scores are more consistent even though they are lower on average."

📝 Worked Example: Comparing

Class A: median=78, IQR=10. Class B: median=85, IQR=15. Compare.

  1. Center: Class B scores higher (median 85 vs 78).
  2. Spread: Class A is more consistent (IQR 10 vs 15).
  3. Conclusion: Class B scores higher on average, but Class A scores are more tightly grouped together.
  4. Note: A student in Class A is more predictable; Class B has more variation.
💡 Remember: Always compare BOTH center (who scored higher?) AND spread (who was more consistent?). Use both to tell the full story!

Lesson 26 • Statistics & Probability

Compound Probability

NY State Standard: 7.SP.8

📌 Learning Objective

Students will find probabilities of compound events using organized lists, sample space tables, and tree diagrams.

Compound Probability

A compound event involves two or more simple events happening together (like flipping a coin AND rolling a die).

Finding the Sample Space

The sample space is the set of ALL possible outcomes.

Organized list: Write out all combinations. Good for small sample spaces.

Tree diagram: Branch out from each event. Total outcomes = product of branches.

Table: Grid with one event across the top and one down the side.

Counting Outcomes

If Event A has m outcomes and Event B has n outcomes, total = m × n.

Coin (2) and die (6): 2 × 6 = 12 total outcomes.

Calculating Probability

P(event) = favorable outcomes / total outcomes

Independent events: P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B).

P(heads AND rolling 3) = ½ × ⅙ = 1/12.

Key Vocabulary

Certain: P = 1. Impossible: P = 0. Equally likely: P = ½.

Probability is always between 0 and 1 (or 0% and 100%).

📝 Worked Example: Compound Events

Spin a spinner with 3 equal sections (R, G, B) and flip a coin. P(Green and Heads)?

  1. Total outcomes: 3 spinner × 2 coin = 6 outcomes.
  2. List them: RH, RT, GH, GT, BH, BT.
  3. Favorable: GH = 1 outcome.
  4. P(Green and Heads) = 1/6.
  5. Check with multiplication: P(G) × P(H) = ⅓ × ½ = 1/6. ✅
💡 Remember: For compound events: list all outcomes OR multiply individual probabilities for independent events!

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: Flip two coins. P(both heads)?

½ × ½ = ¼

Example 2: Roll two dice. Total outcomes?

6 × 6 = 36

Example 3: 3 shirts, 4 pants. Total outfits?

3 × 4 = 12

Example 4: P(rolling 6 twice)?

⅙ × ⅙ = 1/36

Example 5: Bag: 2 red, 3 blue. Pick with replacement twice. P(both red)?

2/5 × 2/5 = 4/25
