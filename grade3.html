Lesson 1 • Operations & Algebraic Thinking

Understanding Multiplication

NY State Standard: 3.OA.1

📌 Learning Objective

Students will interpret products of whole numbers as the total number of objects in equal groups.

What Is Multiplication?

Multiplication means finding the total when you have equal groups.

3 × 4 means "3 groups of 4." That is 4 + 4 + 4 = 12.

Key Vocabulary

Factors: the numbers being multiplied (3 and 4). Product: the answer (12).

You can think of multiplication as repeated addition or as an array (rows and columns).

📝 Worked Example: Equal Groups

There are 5 bags with 3 apples in each. How many apples total?

  1. Identify groups: 5 groups of 3.
  2. Write: 5 × 3 = ?
  3. Add: 3+3+3+3+3 = 15 apples.
💡 Remember: Multiplication = equal groups. Count the groups, count how many in each group!

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: 4 groups of 6?

4 × 6 = 24

Example 2: 2 groups of 9?

2 × 9 = 18

Example 3: 7 × 3 as repeated addition?

3+3+3+3+3+3+3 = 21

Lesson 2 • Operations & Algebraic Thinking

Understanding Division

NY State Standard: 3.OA.2

📌 Learning Objective

Students will interpret quotients as sharing equally or making equal groups.

What Is Division?

Division means splitting a total into equal groups.

12 ÷ 3 = 4 means "12 split into 3 equal groups gives 4 in each group."

It can also mean: "How many groups of 3 are in 12?" Answer: 4 groups.

Key Vocabulary

Dividend: the total (12). Divisor: the number of groups or size of each group (3). Quotient: the answer (4).

📝 Worked Example: Sharing Equally

15 stickers shared equally among 5 friends. How many each?

  1. Total: 15. Groups: 5.
  2. Equation: 15 ÷ 5 = ?
  3. Think: 5 × ? = 15. Since 5 × 3 = 15, answer is 3 stickers each.
💡 Remember: Division is the opposite of multiplication. Use multiplication to check!

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: 20 ÷ 4 = ?

5. Because 4 × 5 = 20. 5

Example 2: 18 ÷ 6 = ?

6 × 3 = 18. 3

Example 3: 24 cookies, 8 per bag?

24 ÷ 8 = 3 bags

Lesson 3 • Operations & Algebraic Thinking

Multiplication & Division Word Problems

NY State Standard: 3.OA.3

📌 Learning Objective

Students will solve multiplication and division word problems within 100.

Word Problems with Multiplication and Division

Multiplication clues: "each," "every," "per," "groups of," "times as many."

Division clues: "share equally," "split," "how many groups," "how many in each."

Read, decide the operation, write an equation, solve, and check.

📝 Worked Example: Word Problem

A bookshelf has 6 shelves with 8 books on each. How many books total?

  1. Groups: 6 shelves. Per group: 8 books.
  2. Multiply: 6 × 8 = 48 books.
💡 Remember: Equal groups = multiply. Sharing equally = divide.

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: 28 chairs in 4 equal rows. How many per row?

28 ÷ 4 = 7 chairs

Example 2: 7 packs, 5 cards each. Total?

7 × 5 = 35 cards

Example 3: 36 students into teams of 9?

36 ÷ 9 = 4 teams

Lesson 4 • Operations & Algebraic Thinking

Finding Unknown Numbers

NY State Standard: 3.OA.4

📌 Learning Objective

Students will determine unknown numbers in multiplication and division equations.

Missing Numbers

Sometimes the unknown is a factor or the product:

? × 6 = 42: Think 42 ÷ 6 = 7. So ? = 7.

8 × ? = 56: Think 56 ÷ 8 = 7. So ? = 7.

48 ÷ ? = 6: Think ? × 6 = 48. So ? = 8.

📝 Worked Example: Finding the Unknown

Solve: ? x 7 = 63

  1. Rewrite: 63 ÷ 7 = ?
  2. Think: 7 × 9 = 63.
  3. Answer: ? = 9.
💡 Remember: Use the inverse operation to find unknowns!

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: 5 x ? = 40

40 ÷ 5 = 8

Example 2: ? x 3 = 27

27 ÷ 3 = 9

Example 3: 72 / ? = 8

? × 8 = 72. ? = 9

Lesson 5 • Operations & Algebraic Thinking

Properties of Multiplication

NY State Standard: 3.OA.5

📌 Learning Objective

Students will apply the commutative, associative, and distributive properties of multiplication.

Three Important Properties

Commutative: Order does not matter. 4 × 7 = 7 × 4.

Associative: Grouping does not matter. (2 × 3) × 4 = 2 × (3 × 4).

Distributive: Break apart a factor. 6 × 8 = 6 × (5+3) = 30 + 18 = 48.

These properties help solve harder problems by breaking them into easier ones.

📝 Worked Example: Distributive Property

Solve 7 x 6 using the distributive property.

  1. Break apart 6: 6 = 5 + 1.
  2. Distribute: 7 × 5 + 7 × 1 = 35 + 7.
  3. Answer: 42.
💡 Remember: Break a hard fact into two easy ones!

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: 4 x 5 = 5 x ?

4 (commutative)

Example 2: (3 x 2) x 5 = 3 x (2 x ?)

5 (associative)

Example 3: 8 x 7 = 8 x 5 + 8 x 2 = ?

40 + 16 = 56

Division as Unknown Factor

NY State Standard: 3.OA.6

📌 Learning Objective

Students will understand division as finding an unknown factor.

Division = Unknown Factor

Every division problem can be written as a multiplication problem with a missing factor.

32 ÷ 8 = ? is the same as 8 × ? = 32. Answer: 4.

If you know your multiplication facts, you can solve any division problem!

📝 Worked Example: Think Multiplication

Solve: 56 / 7 = ?

  1. Rewrite: 7 × ? = 56.
  2. Think: 7 × 8 = 56.
  3. Answer: 56 ÷ 7 = 8.
💡 Remember: Turn every division into multiplication with a missing factor!

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: 42 / 6 = ?

6 × ? = 42. ? = 7

Example 2: 36 / 9 = ?

9 × ? = 36. ? = 4

Example 3: 27 / 3 = ?

3 × ? = 27. ? = 9

📌 Learning Objective

Students will know from memory all products of two one-digit numbers.

Multiplication Fact Fluency

By the end of Grade 3, you should know all multiplication facts from 1 × 1 through 9 × 9 from memory.

Strategies to remember:

• x1: the number stays the same. • x2: doubles. • x5: count by 5s. • x10: add a zero. • x9: the digits of the product add to 9 (e.g. 9x7=63, 6+3=9).

📝 Worked Example: Fast Facts

Solve quickly: 7 x 8, 6 x 9, 4 x 7

  1. 7 x 8 = 56
  2. 6 x 9 = 54
  3. 4 x 7 = 28
💡 Remember: Practice daily! The more automatic these facts are, the easier all math becomes.

Lesson 8 • Operations & Algebraic Thinking

Two-Step Word Problems

NY State Standard: 3.OA.8

📌 Learning Objective

Students will solve two-step word problems using all four operations.

Two-Step Problems with All Operations

These problems require two calculations. The operations could be any mix of addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division.

Steps: 1. Read carefully. 2. Identify step 1. 3. Solve step 1. 4. Use that answer for step 2. 5. Answer the question.

📝 Worked Example: Two Steps

A store has 5 boxes of 8 juice boxes. They sell 13. How many are left?

  1. Step 1 (multiply): 5 × 8 = 40 juice boxes.
  2. Step 2 (subtract): 40 - 13 = 27 juice boxes left.
💡 Remember: Identify which operation comes first!

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: 24 cookies on 3 plates. Eat 5 from one plate. Remaining on that plate?

24/3=8 per plate. 8-5=3

Example 2: Buy 4 packs of 6 pens and 3 single pens. Total?

4x6=24. 24+3=27

Example 3: 45 apples. Give 9 to each of 4 friends. Left?

9x4=36 given. 45-36=9

Lesson 9 • Operations & Algebraic Thinking

Arithmetic Patterns

NY State Standard: 3.OA.9

📌 Learning Objective

Students will identify arithmetic patterns and explain them using properties of operations.

Finding Patterns

Patterns appear everywhere in math. Looking at patterns helps us predict and understand.

Addition patterns: The sum of two even numbers is always even. An even + an odd = odd.

Multiplication patterns: Any number × an even number = even product. Multiples of 5 end in 0 or 5. Multiples of 9: digits add to 9.

📝 Worked Example: Pattern Spotting

What pattern do you see in multiples of 4: 4, 8, 12, 16, 20, 24...?

  1. Look at the ones digits: 4, 8, 2, 6, 0, 4, 8, 2, 6, 0...
  2. Pattern: The ones digits repeat in a cycle of 5: 4, 8, 2, 6, 0.
  3. Also: Each multiple is 4 more than the previous.
💡 Remember: Look at ones digits, differences between numbers, and even/odd patterns!

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: Even + even = ?

Always even (e.g. 4+6=10)

Example 2: Odd x odd = ?

Always odd (e.g. 3x5=15)

Example 3: What do multiples of 10 end in?

Always 0

Lesson 10 • Number & Operations in Base Ten

Rounding to Nearest 10 and 100

NY State Standard: 3.NBT.1

📌 Learning Objective

Students will round whole numbers to the nearest 10 or 100.

Rounding Numbers

Rounding means replacing a number with a nearby "friendly" number.

Rounding to the Nearest 10

Look at the ones digit. If it is 0-4, round down. If it is 5-9, round up. Example: 37 rounds to 40. 32 rounds to 30.

Rounding to the Nearest 100

Look at the tens digit. If it is 0-4, round down. If it is 5-9, round up. Example: 462 rounds to 500. 430 rounds to 400.

📝 Worked Example: Rounding

Round 67 to the nearest 10.

  1. Ones digit: 7.
  2. 7 ≥ 5: Round up.
  3. Answer: 67 rounds to 70.
💡 Remember: 5 or more, round up. 4 or less, round down.

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: Round 34 to nearest 10?

Ones=4, round down: 30

Example 2: Round 851 to nearest 100?

Tens=5, round up: 900

Example 3: Round 245 to nearest 10?

Ones=5, round up: 250

Lesson 11 • Number & Operations in Base Ten

Fluently Add & Subtract Within 1000

NY State Standard: 3.NBT.2

📌 Learning Objective

Students will fluently add and subtract within 1000 using strategies and algorithms.

Adding and Subtracting Within 1000

Use place value to add and subtract three-digit numbers. Work column by column from right to left.

Adding: Add ones, tens, then hundreds. Regroup when a column reaches 10+.

Subtracting: Subtract ones, tens, then hundreds. Borrow when the top digit is smaller.

📝 Worked Example: Column Addition

458 + 376 = ?

  1. Ones: 8+6=14. Write 4, carry 1.
  2. Tens: 5+7+1=13. Write 3, carry 1.
  3. Hundreds: 4+3+1=8.
  4. Answer: 834.
💡 Remember: Work right to left. Regroup at 10 or more.

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: 652 - 287 = ?

Borrow: 12-7=5, 14-8=6 (borrow from hundreds: 5-2=3). 365

Example 2: 309 + 456 = ?

9+6=15, 0+5+1=6, 3+4=7. 765

Example 3: 800 - 253 = ?

547

Multiplying by Multiples of 10

NY State Standard: 3.NBT.3

📌 Learning Objective

Students will multiply one-digit numbers by multiples of 10.

Multiplying by 10, 20, 30...

To multiply a one-digit number by a multiple of 10, multiply the basic fact, then add a zero.

5 × 40: Think 5 × 4 = 20, then add a zero: 200.

Why? 40 is 4 tens. 5 groups of 4 tens = 20 tens = 200.

6 x 70 = ?

  1. Basic fact: 6 × 7 = 42.
  2. Add a zero: 42 → 420.
💡 Remember: Multiply the basic fact, then attach the zero from the multiple of 10!

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: 3 x 50 = ?

3 x 5 = 15, add 0: 150

Example 2: 8 x 20 = ?

8 x 2 = 16, add 0: 160

Example 3: 9 x 30 = ?

9 x 3 = 27, add 0: 270

Lesson 13 • Fractions

Understanding Fractions

NY State Standard: 3.NF.1

📌 Learning Objective

Students will understand a fraction 1/b as one part when a whole is divided into b equal parts.

What Is a Fraction?

A fraction describes parts of a whole. It has a numerator (top) and denominator (bottom).

The denominator tells how many equal parts the whole is divided into. The numerator tells how many parts we are talking about.

Example: 3/4 means the whole is split into 4 equal parts, and we have 3 of them.

A unit fraction has a numerator of 1, like 1/2, 1/3, 1/4.

📝 Worked Example: Reading a Fraction

A pizza is cut into 8 equal slices. You eat 3. What fraction did you eat?

  1. Total slices: 8 (denominator).
  2. Slices eaten: 3 (numerator).
  3. Fraction: 3/8.
💡 Remember: Denominator = total equal parts. Numerator = parts described.

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: Pie cut into 6 pieces, 2 eaten?

2/6

Example 2: Bar split into 4 parts, 1 shaded?

1/4 (a unit fraction)

Example 3: What does the 5 mean in 5/8?

We are talking about 5 parts (numerator)

Lesson 14 • Fractions

Fractions on a Number Line

NY State Standard: 3.NF.2

📌 Learning Objective

Students will represent fractions on a number line between 0 and 1.

Fractions on a Number Line

A number line from 0 to 1 can be divided into equal parts to show fractions.

For fourths: divide the line into 4 equal parts. The marks are at 1/4, 2/4, 3/4, and 4/4 (=1).

The denominator tells how many equal parts. The numerator tells how many parts from 0.

📝 Worked Example: Plotting a Fraction

Place 3/4 on a number line.

  1. Divide 0 to 1 into 4 equal parts.
  2. Count 3 parts from 0: 1/4, 2/4, 3/4.
  3. Mark: 3/4 is 3 jumps from 0.
💡 Remember: The denominator tells the size of each part. The numerator counts how many parts from 0.

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: Where is 1/3 on a number line?

Divide into 3 parts. 1 part from 0.

Example 2: Where is 2/6?

Divide into 6 parts, count 2. Same as 1/3.

Example 3: Which is closer to 1: 2/4 or 3/4?

3/4 (further along)

Lesson 15 • Fractions

Equivalent Fractions

NY State Standard: 3.NF.3

📌 Learning Objective

Students will recognize and generate simple equivalent fractions.

Equivalent Fractions

Equivalent fractions are different fractions that name the same amount.

Example: 1/2 = 2/4 = 3/6. They all represent exactly half.

How to find equivalents: Multiply (or divide) both the numerator and denominator by the same number.

1/2 × 2/2 = 2/4. 1/2 × 3/3 = 3/6.

📝 Worked Example: Finding Equivalents

Find a fraction equivalent to 2/3.

  1. Multiply top and bottom by 2: 2×2=4, 3×2=6.
  2. Answer: 2/3 = 4/6.
💡 Remember: Multiply or divide both top and bottom by the same number!

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: Equivalent to 1/4?

1×2 / 4×2 = 2/8

Example 2: Is 2/4 = 1/2?

Yes! 2/4 simplifies to 1/2.

Example 3: 3/6 simplified?

Divide both by 3: 1/2

Lesson 16 • Fractions

Comparing Fractions

NY State Standard: 3.NF.3

📌 Learning Objective

Students will compare fractions with the same numerator or same denominator.

Comparing Fractions

Same Denominator

When denominators are the same, the fraction with the bigger numerator is greater. 5/8 > 3/8 because 5 parts > 3 parts.

Same Numerator

When numerators are the same, the fraction with the smaller denominator is greater. 1/3 > 1/6 because thirds are bigger pieces than sixths.

📝 Worked Example: Comparing

Which is greater: 3/4 or 3/8?

  1. Same numerator (3).
  2. Compare denominators: 4 vs 8.
  3. Smaller denominator = bigger pieces: 3/4 > 3/8.
💡 Remember: Same denominator: bigger numerator wins. Same numerator: smaller denominator wins.

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: 5/6 vs 2/6?

Same denominator. 5 > 2. 5/6 > 2/6

Example 2: 1/2 vs 1/4?

Same numerator. 2 < 4. 1/2 > 1/4

Example 3: 2/3 vs 2/8?

Same numerator. 3 < 8. 2/3 > 2/8

Whole Numbers as Fractions

NY State Standard: 3.NF.3

📌 Learning Objective

Students will express whole numbers as fractions and recognize that n/1 = n.

Whole Numbers as Fractions

Any whole number can be written as a fraction with denominator 1.

3 = 3/1. This means 3 wholes, each divided into 1 part.

Also: 1 = 2/2 = 3/3 = 4/4 (any number over itself = 1).

And: 2 = 2/1 = 4/2 = 6/3 = 8/4.

📝 Worked Example: Converting

Write 4 as a fraction.

  1. Any whole number over 1: 4 = 4/1.
  2. Or: 4 = 8/2 = 12/3 = 16/4.
💡 Remember: Whole number / 1 = the whole number. Anything / itself = 1.

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: Write 5 as a fraction.

5/1

Example 2: What is 6/6?

1

Example 3: Write 3 as a fraction with denominator 4.

3 = 12/4

Telling Time to the Minute

NY State Standard: 3.MD.1

📌 Learning Objective

Students will tell and write time to the nearest minute.

Reading Minutes Precisely

Each small tick mark on a clock = 1 minute. There are 60 minutes in 1 hour.

Count by 5s to the nearest number, then count individual tick marks.

Example: minute hand past the 4 by 2 tick marks = 4×5 + 2 = 22 minutes.

📝 Worked Example: Reading Time

Hour hand past 8, minute hand on the 7th tick past the 3. Time?

  1. Hour: 8.
  2. Minutes: 3 × 5 = 15, plus 2 more = 17.
  3. Time: 8:17.
💡 Remember: Count by 5s to the nearest number, then count extra ticks by 1s.

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: Hour 10, minute exactly on 6?

10:30

Example 2: Hour between 3 and 4, minute 3 ticks past 11?

3:58. 11x5=55 + 3=58

Example 3: 25 minutes after 7:00?

7:25

Lesson 19 • Measurement & Data

Elapsed Time

NY State Standard: 3.MD.1

📌 Learning Objective

Students will solve word problems involving addition and subtraction of time intervals.

Elapsed Time

Elapsed time is the amount of time that passes between a start time and an end time.

To find elapsed time: count forward from the start to the end.

Example: Start at 2:15, end at 3:45. From 2:15 to 3:15 = 1 hour. From 3:15 to 3:45 = 30 min. Total: 1 hour 30 minutes.

📝 Worked Example: Finding Elapsed Time

A movie starts at 4:10 and ends at 5:40. How long is the movie?

  1. 4:10 to 5:10: 1 hour.
  2. 5:10 to 5:40: 30 minutes.
  3. Total: 1 hour 30 minutes.
💡 Remember: Count hours first, then remaining minutes!

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: Start 8:00, end 8:45?

45 minutes

Example 2: Start 1:30, end 3:00?

1:30 to 3:00 = 1 hour 30 minutes

Example 3: Start 10:15, it takes 25 minutes. End time?

10:15 + 25 min = 10:40

Liquid Volume & Mass

NY State Standard: 3.MD.2

📌 Learning Objective

Students will measure liquid volume in liters and mass in grams and kilograms.

Measuring Volume and Mass

Liquid volume is measured in liters (L). A large water bottle is about 1 liter.

Mass is measured in grams (g) and kilograms (kg). 1 kg = 1000 g.

A paperclip is about 1 gram. A textbook is about 1 kilogram.

📝 Worked Example: Choosing Units

Would you measure a watermelon in grams or kilograms?

  1. A watermelon is heavy (several kg).
  2. Answer: Kilograms is the better unit.
💡 Remember: Small/light = grams. Large/heavy = kilograms. Liquids = liters.

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: Mass of a feather?

Grams (very light)

Example 2: Volume of a fish tank?

Liters (liquid)

Example 3: 1 kg = ? g

1000 grams

Picture & Bar Graphs with Scales

NY State Standard: 3.MD.3

📌 Learning Objective

Students will draw and read scaled picture graphs and bar graphs.

Scaled Graphs

In a scaled graph, each symbol or grid line can represent more than 1.

Example: If each picture = 5 students, and "Soccer" has 4 pictures, that means 4 × 5 = 20 students.

Always check the key or scale before reading the graph!

📝 Worked Example: Reading a Scaled Graph

Each star = 2 votes. Cats have 6 stars. How many votes?

  1. Scale: 1 star = 2 votes.
  2. Count: 6 stars × 2 = 12 votes.
💡 Remember: Always multiply the count by the scale!

🤝 Guided Practice

3 × 5 = 15

Example 2: Bar reaches 40 on a scale of 10s. How many?

40

Example 3: 8 symbols, each = 2. Total?

Lesson 22 • Measurement & Data

Measuring Area

NY State Standard: 3.MD.5

📌 Learning Objective

Students will measure area by counting unit squares.

What Is Area?

Area is the amount of space inside a flat shape, measured in square units.

To find area, count the number of unit squares that fill the shape. Common units: square centimeters (sq cm), square inches (sq in), square feet (sq ft).

For a rectangle, you can count rows × columns instead of counting every square.

📝 Worked Example: Counting Squares

A rectangle is covered by 3 rows of 5 squares each. What is its area?

  1. Count: 3 rows × 5 columns.
  2. Total: 15 squares.
  3. Area: 15 square units.
💡 Remember: Area of a rectangle = rows x columns = length x width.

Area and the Distributive Property

NY State Standard: 3.MD.7

📌 Learning Objective

Students will find area of rectangles using multiplication and the distributive property.

Area = Length × Width

For any rectangle: Area = length × width.

Using the Distributive Property

For harder problems, break one side into parts. A 7 × 8 rectangle can be split into 7 × 5 and 7 × 3.

7 × 5 = 35 and 7 × 3 = 21. Total area: 35 + 21 = 56 square units.

  2. 6 × 5 = 30. 6 × 4 = 24.

Lesson 24 • Measurement & Data

Perimeter

NY State Standard: 3.MD.8

📌 Learning Objective

Students will find the perimeter of polygons and solve perimeter problems.

What Is Perimeter?

Perimeter is the distance around a shape. Add up all the side lengths.

Rectangle perimeter: P = 2 × length + 2 × width (or add all four sides).

Square perimeter: P = 4 × side length (all sides are equal).

📝 Worked Example: Finding Perimeter

A rectangle is 8 cm long and 5 cm wide. What is its perimeter?

  1. Add all sides: 8 + 5 + 8 + 5.
  2. Or use formula: 2 × 8 + 2 × 5 = 16 + 10.
  3. Perimeter: 26 cm.
💡 Remember: Perimeter = total distance around. Add ALL sides!

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: Square with side 6?

4 × 6 = 24

Example 2: Triangle: sides 3, 4, 5?

3 + 4 + 5 = 12

Example 3: Rectangle 10 by 3?

2(10) + 2(3) = 20 + 6 = 26

Lesson 25 • Geometry

Understanding Quadrilaterals

NY State Standard: 3.G.1

📌 Learning Objective

Students will classify quadrilaterals by their attributes.

Quadrilaterals

A quadrilateral is any shape with 4 sides and 4 angles.

Types:

• Rectangle: 4 right angles. Opposite sides equal.

• Square: 4 right angles AND 4 equal sides (special rectangle).

• Rhombus: 4 equal sides (not necessarily right angles).

• Trapezoid: Exactly 1 pair of parallel sides.

• Parallelogram: 2 pairs of parallel sides.

📝 Worked Example: Classifying

A shape has 4 right angles and 2 pairs of sides: 2 long, 2 short. What is it?

  1. 4 sides: quadrilateral.
  2. 4 right angles: rectangle.
  3. Sides not all equal: rectangle (not a square).
  4. Answer: Rectangle.
💡 Remember: All squares are rectangles, but not all rectangles are squares!

Square

Example 2: 4 sides, 1 pair of parallel sides?

Lesson 26 • Geometry

Partitioning Shapes into Equal Areas

NY State Standard: 3.G.2

📌 Learning Objective

Students will partition shapes into parts with equal areas and express each part as a fraction of the whole.

Equal Areas as Fractions

When you partition (divide) a shape into equal-sized parts, each part has the same area.

If a rectangle is divided into 6 equal parts, each part is 1/6 of the total area.

The total area of all parts always equals the whole (1).

Different shapes can be divided in different ways, but the parts must be equal in area.

📝 Worked Example: Fractions of Area

A rectangle is divided into 8 equal parts. What fraction is each part? What fraction is 3 parts?

  1. Each part: 1/8 of the whole area.
  2. 3 parts: 3/8 of the whole area.
💡 Remember: Number of parts = denominator. Number of parts you are looking at = numerator.

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: Square divided into 4 parts. Each part?

1/4

Example 2: Circle into 3 parts, 2 shaded?

2/3

Example 3: Rectangle into 6 parts. All 6?

6/6 = 1 whole
