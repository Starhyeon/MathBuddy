Lesson 1 • Operations & Algebraic Thinking

Multiplicative Comparisons

NY State Standard: 4.OA.1

📌 Learning Objective

Students will interpret a multiplication equation as a comparison (e.g., 35 = 5 x 7 means 35 is 5 times as many as 7).

What Is a Multiplicative Comparison?

A multiplicative comparison uses "times as many" or "times as much."

35 = 5 × 7 means "35 is 5 times as many as 7."

This is different from additive comparison ("8 more than"), which uses addition.

📝 Worked Example: Comparison

Sam has 4 cards. Mia has 5 times as many. How many does Mia have?

  1. Comparison: 5 times as many as 4.
  2. Equation: 5 × 4 = ?
  3. Answer: Mia has 20 cards.
💡 Remember: Times as many = multiply!

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: 3 times as many as 6?

3 × 6 = 18

Example 2: 24 is 4 times as many as what?

24 ÷ 4 = 6

Example 3: 8 is ? times as many as 2

8 ÷ 2 = 4 times

Lesson 2 • Operations & Algebraic Thinking

Comparison Word Problems

NY State Standard: 4.OA.2

📌 Learning Objective

Students will solve multiplicative comparison word problems.

Solving Comparison Word Problems

Identify the multiplier, the smaller amount, and the larger amount.

If you know the multiplier and smaller: multiply. If you know the larger and multiplier: divide.

📝 Worked Example: Word Problem

A dog weighs 8 times as much as a cat. The cat weighs 6 pounds. How much does the dog weigh?

  1. Multiplier: 8 times.
  2. Smaller: 6 pounds (cat).
  3. Equation: 8 × 6 = 48 pounds.
💡 Remember: Find the multiplier and decide: multiply or divide.

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: Ana has 3 times as many books as Ben. Ben has 12. Ana?

3 × 12 = 36

Example 2: A table is 4 times as long as a chair. Table: 32 in. Chair?

32 ÷ 4 = 8 in

Example 3: Lisa has 45 stickers, 9 times as many as Jay. Jay?

45 ÷ 9 = 5

Lesson 3 • Operations & Algebraic Thinking

Multi-Step Word Problems

NY State Standard: 4.OA.3

📌 Learning Objective

Students will solve multi-step word problems using all four operations and assess reasonableness.

Multi-Step Problems

These require two or more steps with any combination of +, -, ×, ÷.

Steps: 1. Read carefully. 2. Plan which operations. 3. Solve step by step. 4. Check if the answer is reasonable.

Estimating: Round numbers to check if your exact answer makes sense.

📝 Worked Example: Multi-Step

A store sells 6 packs of 12 pencils and 4 single pencils. How many pencils total?

  1. Step 1: 6 × 12 = 72 pencils in packs.
  2. Step 2: 72 + 4 = 76 pencils total.
  3. Check: 6 × 10 = 60, + 4 = 64. Our answer 76 is reasonable.
💡 Remember: Always estimate to check your answer!

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: Buy 3 books at $8 each and pay with $30. Change?

3x8=24. 30-24=$6

Example 2: 240 students in 8 classes. 15 absent today. How many present?

240/8=30 per class is extra info. 240-15=225

Example 3: 4 bags of 15 apples. Give 20 away. Remaining?

4x15=60. 60-20=40

Lesson 4 • Operations & Algebraic Thinking

Factor Pairs & Prime/Composite

NY State Standard: 4.OA.4

📌 Learning Objective

Students will find factor pairs and determine if numbers are prime or composite.

Factors and Factor Pairs

A factor is a number that divides evenly into another number. Factor pairs are two numbers that multiply to give a product.

Factors of 12: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12. Factor pairs: (1,12), (2,6), (3,4).

Prime vs. Composite

Prime: Exactly 2 factors (1 and itself). Examples: 2, 3, 5, 7, 11.

Composite: More than 2 factors. Examples: 4, 6, 8, 9, 10.

1 is neither prime nor composite.

📝 Worked Example: Finding Factors

Find all factor pairs of 24.

  1. Start with 1: 1 × 24.
  2. Try 2: 2 × 12.
  3. Try 3: 3 × 8.
  4. Try 4: 4 × 6.
  5. Factors: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, 24.
💡 Remember: Start at 1 and work up. Stop when you reach a pair you already found.

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: Is 7 prime or composite?

Prime (only 1 and 7)

Example 2: Factors of 18?

1, 2, 3, 6, 9, 18

Example 3: Is 15 prime or composite?

Composite (1, 3, 5, 15)

Number & Shape Patterns

NY State Standard: 4.OA.5

📌 Learning Objective

Students will generate and analyze number or shape patterns following a given rule.

Patterns and Rules

A pattern follows a rule. Given a rule, you can generate the pattern and observe features.

Rule: "Start at 3, add 6." Pattern: 3, 9, 15, 21, 27...

Observations: All numbers are odd. Each number is 6 more than the last.

📝 Worked Example: Generating a Pattern

Rule: Start at 1, multiply by 2. Give the first 6 terms.

  1. Start: 1.
  2. Apply rule: 1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32.
  3. Observation: Each term doubles. All are powers of 2.
💡 Remember: Apply the rule step by step and look for features!

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: Start at 5, add 3. Next 4 terms?

5, 8, 11, 14, 17

Example 2: Start at 0, add 4. Even or odd?

All even: 0,4,8,12...

2, 6, 18, 54

Lesson 6 • Number & Operations in Base Ten

Place Value to 1,000,000

NY State Standard: 4.NBT.1

📌 Learning Objective

Students will recognize that a digit in one place represents 10 times what it represents in the place to its right.

Place Value Relationships

Each place value is 10 times the place to its right.

In 444: the first 4 = 400 (hundreds), the second 4 = 40 (tens), the third 4 = 4 (ones). Each 4 is worth 10 times the 4 to its right.

Places: ones, tens, hundreds, thousands, ten thousands, hundred thousands, millions.

📝 Worked Example: Place Value

In 55,555, how many times greater is the 5 in the ten-thousands place than the 5 in the thousands place?

  1. Ten-thousands 5: 50,000.
  2. Thousands 5: 5,000.
  3. Compare: 50,000 ÷ 5,000 = 10 times.
💡 Remember: Each move left is x10. Each move right is /10.

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: Value of 7 in 73,000?

70,000

Example 2: 3 in 300 is ___ times 3 in 30?

10 times

Example 3: What place is the 6 in 642,000?

Hundred thousands

Lesson 8 • Number & Operations in Base Ten

Rounding Multi-Digit Numbers

NY State Standard: 4.NBT.3

📌 Learning Objective

Students will round multi-digit whole numbers to any place.

Rounding to Any Place

Steps: 1. Find the rounding place. 2. Look at the digit to its right. 3. If 0-4, round down. If 5-9, round up. 4. Replace all digits to the right with zeros.

Round 45,678 to the nearest thousand: look at hundreds digit (6). 6 ≥ 5, so round up: 46,000.

📝 Worked Example: Rounding

Round 372,849 to the nearest ten thousand.

  1. Ten-thousands digit: 7.
  2. Look right (thousands): 2.
  3. 2 < 5: Round down.
  4. Answer: 370,000.
💡 Remember: Underline the rounding digit. Circle the digit to its right. That digit decides!

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: Round 8,452 to nearest hundred?

Tens=5, round up: 8,500

Example 2: Round 65,321 to nearest ten thousand?

Thousands=5, round up: 70,000

Example 3: Round 999 to nearest hundred?

1,000

Lesson 9 • Number & Operations in Base Ten

Fluent Multi-Digit Addition & Subtraction

NY State Standard: 4.NBT.4

📌 Learning Objective

Students will fluently add and subtract multi-digit whole numbers using the standard algorithm.

Standard Algorithm

Line up digits by place value. Work right to left.

Addition: Add each column. Carry when the sum is 10+.

Subtraction: Subtract each column. Borrow when the top is smaller.

📝 Worked Example: Addition

34,567 + 28,945 = ?

  1. Ones: 7+5=12, write 2 carry 1.
  2. Tens: 6+4+1=11, write 1 carry 1.
  3. Hundreds: 5+9+1=15, write 5 carry 1.
  4. Thousands: 4+8+1=13, write 3 carry 1.
  5. Ten-thousands: 3+2+1=6. Answer: 63,512.
💡 Remember: Align digits carefully. Regroup as needed.

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: 52,000 - 18,347 = ?

33,653

Example 2: 100,000 - 1 = ?

99,999

Example 3: 45,678 + 54,322 = ?

100,000

Lesson 10 • Number & Operations in Base Ten

Multiplying Whole Numbers

NY State Standard: 4.NBT.5

📌 Learning Objective

Students will multiply up to 4-digit by 1-digit and 2-digit by 2-digit numbers.

Multi-Digit Multiplication

4-digit by 1-digit

Multiply each digit by the one-digit number, regrouping as needed. Example: 2,345 × 3 = 7,035.

2-digit by 2-digit

Use partial products: 23 × 45 = (23×40) + (23×5) = 920 + 115 = 1,035.

Or use the standard algorithm: multiply by ones, then by tens (shift left), then add.

📝 Worked Example: 2-digit by 2-digit

34 x 27 = ?

  1. 34 × 7 = 238 (partial product 1).
  2. 34 × 20 = 680 (partial product 2).
  3. Add: 238 + 680 = 918.
💡 Remember: Break the problem into partial products and add them up!

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: 156 x 4 = ?

624

Example 2: 52 x 13 = ?

52x10=520, 52x3=156. 520+156=676

Example 3: 1,234 x 2 = ?

2,468

Lesson 11 • Number & Operations in Base Ten

Dividing by One-Digit Numbers

NY State Standard: 4.NBT.6

📌 Learning Objective

Students will divide up to 4-digit dividends by 1-digit divisors, finding quotients and remainders.

Long Division

Use the divide-multiply-subtract-bring down cycle.

Example: 845 ÷ 5. 5 goes into 8 once (1), 8-5=3. Bring down 4: 34. 5 goes into 34 six times (6), 34-30=4. Bring down 5: 45. 5 goes into 45 nine times (9). Answer: 169.

If there is a remainder, write it as R. Example: 23 ÷ 5 = 4 R3.

📝 Worked Example: Long Division

732 / 4 = ?

  1. 4 into 7: 1 time. 7-4=3.
  2. Bring down 3: 33. 4 into 33: 8 times. 33-32=1.
  3. Bring down 2: 12. 4 into 12: 3 times. 12-12=0.
  4. Answer: 183.
💡 Remember: Divide-Multiply-Subtract-Bring down. Repeat!

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: 96 / 8 = ?

12

Example 2: 257 / 3 = ?

85 R2

Example 3: 1,000 / 5 = ?

200

Lesson 12 • Fractions & Decimals

Equivalent Fractions

NY State Standard: 4.NF.1

📌 Learning Objective

Students will explain and generate equivalent fractions using visual fraction models.

Creating Equivalent Fractions

Multiply or divide both the numerator and denominator by the same number.

2/3 = 4/6 = 6/9 = 8/12 (multiply by 2/2, 3/3, 4/4).

To simplify, divide both by their greatest common factor (GCF).

8/12: GCF of 8 and 12 is 4. 8÷4=2, 12÷4=3. So 8/12 = 2/3.

📝 Worked Example: Generating Equivalents

Write 3 fractions equivalent to 1/4.

  1. Multiply by 2/2: 2/8.
  2. Multiply by 3/3: 3/12.
  3. Multiply by 4/4: 4/16.
💡 Remember: Whatever you do to the top, do the same to the bottom!

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: Simplify 6/8.

GCF=2. 3/4.

Example 2: Is 3/5 = 9/15?

3x3=9, 5x3=15. Yes!

Example 3: Find equivalent of 5/6 with denominator 12.

5x2=10, 6x2=12. 10/12.

Lesson 13 • Fractions & Decimals

Comparing Fractions

NY State Standard: 4.NF.2

📌 Learning Objective

Students will compare fractions with different numerators and different denominators.

Comparing Fractions

To compare fractions with different denominators, find a common denominator.

Compare 3/4 and 5/6: common denominator = 12. 3/4 = 9/12. 5/6 = 10/12. Since 9 < 10, 3/4 < 5/6.

Benchmark strategy: Compare each fraction to 1/2 or 1.

📝 Worked Example: Comparing

Compare 2/3 and 3/5.

  1. Common denominator: 15.
  2. Convert: 2/3 = 10/15. 3/5 = 9/15.
  3. Compare: 10 > 9. So 2/3 > 3/5.
💡 Remember: Same denominator makes comparing easy. Or use benchmarks like 1/2.

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: Compare 1/3 and 1/4?

Common denom 12: 4/12 vs 3/12. 1/3 > 1/4

Example 2: Compare 5/8 and 1/2?

1/2 = 4/8. 5/8 > 4/8. 5/8 > 1/2

Example 3: Compare 7/10 and 3/5?

3/5 = 6/10. 7/10 > 6/10. 7/10 > 3/5

Lesson 14 • Fractions & Decimals

Adding Fractions

NY State Standard: 4.NF.3

📌 Learning Objective

Students will add fractions and mixed numbers with like denominators.

Adding Fractions with Like Denominators

When denominators are the same, add the numerators and keep the denominator.

2/5 + 1/5 = 3/5. The denominator stays 5.

Adding Mixed Numbers

Add whole numbers and fractions separately. 2 1/4 + 1 2/4 = 3 3/4.

If the fraction part is improper, regroup: 3 5/4 = 4 1/4.

📝 Worked Example: Adding Mixed Numbers

3 2/6 + 2 5/6 = ?

  1. Whole numbers: 3+2=5.
  2. Fractions: 2/6+5/6=7/6.
  3. Regroup: 7/6=1 1/6. So 5+1 1/6=6 1/6.
💡 Remember: Add numerators, keep denominator. Regroup if the fraction is improper!

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: 1/4 + 2/4 = ?

3/4

Example 2: 5/8 + 7/8 = ?

12/8 = 1 4/8 = 1 1/2

Example 3: 1 3/5 + 2 4/5 = ?

3 + 7/5 = 4 2/5

Lesson 15 • Fractions & Decimals

Subtracting Fractions

NY State Standard: 4.NF.3

📌 Learning Objective

Students will subtract fractions and mixed numbers with like denominators.

Subtracting Fractions with Like Denominators

Subtract the numerators, keep the denominator. 5/8 - 3/8 = 2/8 = 1/4.

Subtracting Mixed Numbers

Subtract whole numbers and fractions separately. If the fraction part cannot be subtracted, borrow 1 from the whole number.

4 1/6 - 1 5/6: Borrow 1 from 4: 3 7/6 - 1 5/6 = 2 2/6 = 2 1/3.

📝 Worked Example: Subtracting Mixed Numbers

5 2/8 - 2 6/8 = ?

  1. Cannot subtract 6/8 from 2/8.
  2. Borrow: 5 2/8 = 4 10/8.
  3. Subtract: 4 10/8 - 2 6/8 = 2 4/8 = 2 1/2.
💡 Remember: If the top fraction is smaller, borrow 1 whole and convert to an improper fraction.

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: 7/8 - 3/8 = ?

4/8 = 1/2

Example 2: 3 - 1/4 = ?

2 4/4 - 1/4 = 2 3/4

Example 3: 6 1/3 - 2 2/3 = ?

5 4/3 - 2 2/3 = 3 2/3

Lesson 18 • Fractions & Decimals

Comparing Decimals

NY State Standard: 4.NF.7

📌 Learning Objective

Students will compare two decimals to hundredths by reasoning about their size.

Comparing Decimals

Compare decimals by looking at each place from left to right, just like whole numbers.

Compare 0.45 and 0.39: tenths 4 > 3, so 0.45 > 0.39.

If tenths are equal, compare hundredths. 0.52 vs 0.57: tenths same, 2 < 7, so 0.52 < 0.57.

Tip: 0.5 = 0.50. Add a zero to make comparison easier.

📝 Worked Example: Comparing

Compare 0.6 and 0.58.

  1. Rewrite: 0.6 = 0.60.
  2. Compare tenths: Both 6. Equal.
  3. Compare hundredths: 0 vs 8. Wait, 0.60 vs 0.58. 60 hundredths > 58.
  4. Answer: 0.6 > 0.58.
💡 Remember: Make both decimals have the same number of digits, then compare!

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: 0.3 vs 0.29?

0.30 vs 0.29. 0.3 > 0.29

Example 2: 0.75 vs 0.8?

0.75 vs 0.80. 0.75 < 0.8

Example 3: 1.5 vs 1.50?

Equal

Measurement Conversions

NY State Standard: 4.MD.1

📌 Learning Objective

Students will convert measurements within a single system of measurement.

Converting Measurements

Length: 1 ft = 12 in. 1 yd = 3 ft. 1 mi = 5,280 ft. 1 m = 100 cm. 1 km = 1,000 m.

Weight/Mass: 1 lb = 16 oz. 1 kg = 1,000 g.

Capacity: 1 gal = 4 qt. 1 qt = 2 pt. 1 pt = 2 cups. 1 L = 1,000 mL.

Time: 1 hr = 60 min. 1 min = 60 sec. 1 day = 24 hr.

Larger to smaller: multiply. Smaller to larger: divide.

📝 Worked Example: Converting

How many inches in 3 feet?

  1. Conversion: 1 ft = 12 in.
  2. Multiply: 3 × 12 = 36 inches.
💡 Remember: Larger to smaller unit = multiply. Smaller to larger = divide.

🤝 Guided Practice

5 × 3 = 15 ft

Example 2: 48 oz = ? lb

48 ÷ 16 = 3 lb

Example 3: 2 hours = ? minutes

Lesson 20 • Measurement & Data

Measurement Word Problems

NY State Standard: 4.MD.2

📌 Learning Objective

Students will solve word problems involving distances, time, liquid volume, mass, and money with fractions/decimals.

Measurement Word Problems

These combine measurement conversions with problem-solving.

Key: Convert to the same unit before adding, subtracting, or comparing.

📝 Worked Example: Word Problem

A recipe needs 3 cups of flour. You have 1 pint. Do you have enough?

  1. Convert: 1 pint = 2 cups.
  2. Compare: 2 cups < 3 cups.
  3. Answer: No, you need 1 more cup.
💡 Remember: Convert to the same units first!

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: A board is 5 ft. You need 50 inches. Enough?

5x12=60 in. 60>50. Yes!

Example 2: You run 2 km, then 500 m more. Total in meters?

Lesson 21 • Measurement & Data

Area & Perimeter

NY State Standard: 4.MD.3

📌 Learning Objective

Students will apply area and perimeter formulas for rectangles in real-world problems.

Area and Perimeter Formulas

Area = length × width (measured in square units).

Perimeter = 2 × length + 2 × width (or add all sides).

Given area and one side, you can find the other: width = area ÷ length.

Given perimeter and one side: the other side = (perimeter - 2×known side) ÷ 2.

📝 Worked Example: Finding Missing Sides

A rectangle has area 48 sq ft and length 8 ft. What is the width?

  1. Formula: Area = L × W.
  2. Solve: 48 = 8 × W.
  3. W = 48 ÷ 8 = 6 ft.
💡 Remember: Use formulas flexibly: rearrange to find the unknown!

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: Area of 9 by 7?

Example 2: Perimeter of 12 by 5?

Example 3: Perimeter = 30, length = 10. Width?

(30-20)/2 = 5

Line Plots with Fractions

Line Plots with Fractional Data

A line plot uses Xs or dots above a number line to show data.

Data might include fractions like 1/4, 1/2, 3/4. Each measurement gets an X above its value on the number line.

You can find totals, ranges, and most common measurements from a line plot.

Lesson 23 • Measurement & Data

Angles & Degrees

NY State Standard: 4.MD.5

📌 Learning Objective

Students will recognize angles as geometric shapes and understand degree measurement.

Understanding Angles

An angle is formed by two rays sharing a common endpoint (vertex).

Angles are measured in degrees (°). A full turn = 360°. A half turn = 180°. A quarter turn = 90°.

Right angle: exactly 90°. Acute: less than 90°. Obtuse: between 90° and 180°. Straight: exactly 180°.

📝 Worked Example: Classifying Angles

Classify a 120 degree angle.

  1. Compare to 90°: 120 > 90.
  2. Compare to 180°: 120 < 180.
  3. Type: Obtuse.
💡 Remember: Right=90. Acute<90. Obtuse is between 90 and 180. Straight=180.

Lesson 24 • Measurement & Data

Measuring & Drawing Angles

NY State Standard: 4.MD.6

📌 Learning Objective

Students will measure and draw angles using a protractor.

Using a Protractor

To measure: 1. Place the center on the vertex. 2. Align one ray with the 0° line. 3. Read where the other ray crosses the scale.

To draw: 1. Draw a ray. 2. Place protractor at the endpoint. 3. Mark the desired degree. 4. Connect.

Tip: A protractor has two scales. Use the one that starts at 0 on your ray.

📝 Worked Example: Measuring

You measure an angle and the ray crosses at 65 degrees. What type is it?

  1. Read: 65°.
  2. Classify: 65 < 90 = acute angle.
💡 Remember: Always start reading from 0 on the side where your ray is!

Points, Lines, Rays & Angles

NY State Standard: 4.G.1

📌 Learning Objective

Students will draw and identify points, lines, line segments, rays, and angle types.

Basic Geometric Elements

Point: An exact location, shown as a dot. Named with a capital letter (Point A).

Line: Extends forever in both directions. Has arrows on both ends.

Line segment: Part of a line with two endpoints.

Ray: Starts at one point and extends forever in one direction.

Parallel lines: Never cross. Perpendicular lines: Cross at 90°.

📝 Worked Example: Identifying

Two lines cross at a right angle. What are they called?

  1. They cross: They are intersecting.
  2. At 90 degrees: They are perpendicular.
💡 Remember: Parallel = never cross. Perpendicular = cross at right angles.

Lesson 26 • Geometry

Classifying Triangles & Symmetry

NY State Standard: 4.G.2

📌 Learning Objective

Students will classify triangles by angles and sides, and identify lines of symmetry.

Classifying Triangles

By angles: Acute (all angles < 90°), Right (one angle = 90°), Obtuse (one angle > 90°).

By sides: Equilateral (all sides equal), Isosceles (2 sides equal), Scalene (no sides equal).

Lines of Symmetry

A line of symmetry divides a shape into two identical halves that mirror each other.

A square has 4 lines of symmetry. A rectangle has 2. An equilateral triangle has 3.

📝 Worked Example: Classifying a Triangle

A triangle has sides 5, 5, and 8, with all angles less than 90 degrees. Classify it.

  1. By sides: Two sides equal (5 and 5). Isosceles.
  2. By angles: All less than 90. Acute.
  3. Full name: Acute isosceles triangle.
💡 Remember: Check both sides AND angles for full classification!

🤝 Guided Practice

Example 1: All sides 6 cm, all angles 60 degrees?

Equilateral acute triangle

Example 2: One angle is 90, sides 3, 4, 5?

Right scalene triangle

Example 3: How many lines of symmetry in a circle?

Infinite
